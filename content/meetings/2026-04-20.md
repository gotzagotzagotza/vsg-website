## Artist Presentation: Maren Götzmann

Maren Götzmann, a Brisbane and Berlin based visual artist, presented her practice. She works across drawing, printmaking, artist books, and textiles — often combining them into what she describes as drawing with threads and fabric. Her practice explores concepts of dis/placement, migration, and the positioning of humans within the world. She often works in collaboration with other artists to critique an art market that frequently focuses on individual success and commercial value.

She trained in Munich and then completed a second arts degree in Australia after migrating in 1991.

Her central recurring theme is belonging and standpoint — how people position themselves in the world geographically, culturally, and philosophically, particularly as a person who has moved between countries. She referenced the German concept of *Heimat* (which she noted is not directly translatable into English) as a recurring subject.

**Key works shown:**

- **Lifelines** (approx. 20 years ago) — hand-stitched appliqués presented at Artisan Gallery, Brisbane.
- **Touched** (2020) — a quilt-form work made from pre-used intimate clothing (nightgowns, tablecloths, bedsheets) contributed by female friends. Several of those friends had died, making the work deeply personal.
- **Berlin Diaries** (2014–ongoing) — small-format daily visual diary entries made during her time in Berlin, combining print, watercolour, wash, pencil, and collage.
- **Standlines/Standpoints** — a series made both literally (lines and points on paper) and conceptually, including a performance-based ongoing work where she photographs herself within a circle of silk string wherever she is, to mark her position.
- **Kimono wearable art** — collaborative wearable art pieces made with another textile artist from repurposed kimono fabric, sold in gallery settings.

She also presented the work of **Night Letters**, a collective of five artists who have worked together for many years. They meet weekly to collaborate on artist books — where each member works into the others' pages sequentially, without ownership of individual contributions. The process requires leaving ego at the door, as previous work can be entirely covered by the next artist. The collective shows every two years, a cycle they have found sustainable. Their recent series explored the myth of the Australian landscape.

Maren emphasised the importance of artist talks and accessible presentation — allowing audiences to handle and view artist books, and explaining processes — as ways to help the public engage more deeply with work they might otherwise find opaque.

The meeting closed with an open invitation for Maren to return and present more fully, and a suggestion that a similar collaborative artist book practice might be adapted for VSG in some digital form.
